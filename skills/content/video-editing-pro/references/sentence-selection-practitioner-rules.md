## René Coronado's core framework

Coronado teaches a "clarity-of-thought" frame rather than a "duration-reduction" frame.

Direct quote: **"My goal is NOT to get from 14 seconds to 6 seconds."**

His actual goal: reveal the clearest version of the speaker's thought, removing what obscures it.

**What this means for selection:**
The test for each sentence is not "can I cut this to save time?" but "does removing this sentence make the thought clearer or less clear?"

**What he always cuts:**
- Vocal hesitations: um, uh
- Stalling phrases: "kind of," "sort of," "I mean," "you know"
- Repeated words and vocal misdirections
- "Content that obscures the thought"

**Hard rule:** "I never end up cutting anything the guest was actually saying." — he only cuts around content, not the content itself.

**What he always keeps:**
- Breaths that convey "context of the thought"
- Pauses that help the audience process complex information
- Crosstalk and jokes that keep conversation natural
- Any edit where "the edit would call more attention to itself than the offending word"

## The 3-view rule (Rachel Corbett)

Direct quote: **"When you're editing you're probably going to be listening to a piece of audio at least three times and if on the third time something isn't as compelling as it was when you listened to it the first time, cut it out."**

This is a purely practical rule: your audience gets one viewing with fresh eyes. You have heard this material many times. If your own enthusiasm degrades on repeated viewing, a stranger's attention will drop even faster.

Corollary: **"Ideally you only want content in your show that's compelling no matter how many times you listen to it."**

## The selective filler removal rule (podmuse.com)

Direct quote: **"A better standard is selective removal. Cut fillers that block clarity, stack up awkwardly, or interrupt momentum. Leave the ones that sound human and disappear in context."**

This contradicts the common advice to "remove all ums and ahs." Over-cleaning produces speech that sounds "assembled instead of spoken."

Decision tree:
1. Does this filler BLOCK comprehension? → CUT
2. Does it stack with other fillers ("um... you know... like")? → CUT the stack, leave one if it's natural
3. Does it disappear in normal listening? → LEAVE IT
4. Does it make the speaker sound nervous/unprepared (vs natural)? → CUT it

## What to cut first (priority order)

1. Over-affirmations ("exactly!", "absolutely!", "great question!")
2. Restatements (saying the same thing twice in different words)
3. Digressions (side thoughts that don't return to the main point)
4. Connector phrases that add no information ("and so what that means is...", "you know what I mean")
5. Long setup before the actual answer
6. Sentence-final trailing-off ("and yeah, so... you know")
7. Redundant list items (when 3 items say the same thing as 2)

## The "lost the thread" test

From Buzzsprout content editing podcast: editors spot when "podcasts get so open-ended that they've lost the thread." The edit should identify and cut the moment the speaker loses the main point.

Operationally: after each sentence, ask "what is the main point of this clip?" If the current sentence does not serve that point, it is a thread-loss candidate.

---

## NPR rule on reordering

It is acceptable to reorder sentences (move an answer earlier) if it makes the piece clearer — **as long as the meaning is not changed** and no earlier sentence contains "first-reference context" that the moved sentence depends on.

Do not move sentence B before sentence A if sentence B uses a term that sentence A explains. The audience needs the explanation first.
