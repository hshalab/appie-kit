## The four review passes (in order)

### Pass 1: First-view markers
Watch the whole cut with audio, without stopping. Use markers to note first reactions: where you felt bored, where energy spiked, where you felt a moment was unclear. These are your most honest signals — you have seen this material many times, so any moment that still registers as weak is extremely weak to a first-time viewer.

Film Editing Pro: "There's only one time you see the footage like your audience does: the first time."

### Pass 2: Audio-off (primary diagnostic)
Mute all audio. Watch the cut with only captions and visuals.

What you are testing: can a viewer follow the story without sound? Does the energy structure read visually?

Hitchcock's formulation: **"If it's a good cut, the sound could go off, and the audience would still have a perfectly clear idea of what was going on."**

Failures this catches:
- Captions carrying all the narrative weight (visuals are static/weak)
- Energy plateaus with no visual change
- Cut points that look wrong even though they sound fine

### Pass 3: Black-and-white
Apply a desaturation/grayscale effect. Watch without color.

What you are testing: does the energy hierarchy still read? Is the highest-energy segment visually busiest?

Joe Walker (editor of Dune, Arrival, Blade Runner 2049) uses this technique. The absence of color forces you to evaluate composition, motion, and pacing without the emotional bias of color grading.

### Pass 4: Stranger test
Show the cut to someone who has NOT seen the raw material. Do not tell them what to look for. Watch their face during playback, not the screen.

The moments they:
- Look at their phone → this segment lost them
- Zone out → pacing failed here
- Lean forward → this is working
- Laugh or react → emotional peak confirmed

From joelv.ca: **"Watching the cut with someone else beside you is watching the cut with fresh eyes."**
This is described as more useful than any other review technique because it provides unfiltered behavioral data without the other person having to articulate feedback.

## The 3-view rule

Rachel Corbett (podcast editing): if a segment feels less compelling on the third viewing than it did on the first, cut it.

Your audience will see it once. If your own enthusiasm wanes with repetition, the segment does not have enough intrinsic quality to justify its runtime.

## Diagnosing problem areas from the retention curve

Once published, the TikTok/Instagram/YouTube analytics drop-off graph maps every viewer exit to a timestamp. Process:

1. Find the largest drop-off point
2. Scrub to that exact timestamp in your edit
3. The cause is almost always visible in a single frame: a talking-head cut, a text card reading too slowly, or a visual pause

Aibrify: "Every dip on the curve corresponds to a specific second. Scrub the video to that moment — the cause is almost always visible in a single frame."

Use this as a post-publish feedback loop. Map retention drops to specific editing decisions and correct them in the next clip.

## The 5-layer attention model (We Design Motion)

When diagnosing why a cut feels weak even after fixes, use this hierarchy:

| Layer | What viewer feels | What to check |
|---|---|---|
| Promise | "This is for me" | Hook, first line, first visual — is it specific enough? |
| Clarity | "I understand" | Structure, titles, visual hierarchy — too many ideas at once? |
| Progression | "Something's happening" | Beats, escalation — is there a peak to push toward? |
| Contrast | "Now I'm awake again" | Switches in pace, scale, framing, sound — is contrast meaningful? |
| Craft | "This is worth my time" | Typography, motion, color, sound polish — is any element sloppy? |

A cut that fails at layer 1 (Promise) cannot be saved by strong Craft. Fix in order from top to bottom.

## Pattern interrupt frequency (prepublish.ai data)

For 60-second content:
- First 60 seconds: 2–3 interrupts, aggressive pacing
- After minute 1: 1 interrupt per 60–90 seconds

**Diminishing returns: the 4-interrupt ceiling**
Data from PrePublish: "interrupt effectiveness drops significantly after the 4th interrupt in any 5-minute window." More than 4 interrupts in 5 minutes starts to feel hyperactive and erodes trust.

The tell: if you need more than 4 interrupts to hold attention, the problem is not insufficient interrupts — it is insufficient content. Revisit the script/structure.

Warning on artificial energy: "The Energy Spike — suddenly and dramatically increasing your energy level... Artificial enthusiasm triggers the viewer's BS detector. If the energy level of the interrupt doesn't match the value of the content that follows, the viewer loses trust. Low-trust viewers leave."
